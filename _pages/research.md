**Working Papers**
- Experience, Conscious Control and Deepfakes in nonverbal communication at FOMC press conferences
  - Presented at Baruch College Workshop
  - Abstract: This paper investigates the impact of the Federal Reserve (Fed) Chair's facial expressions during FOMC press conferences on market outcomes, examining whether these nonverbal cues are consciously used to influence markets and if experience enhances a Chair's control over these expressions to promote stability. I use facial emotional recognition software and neural network deepfake technology to study the Chair's information delivery. I find that as the Fed Chair's term lengthens, they relax more into the role and make more negative expressions, but these negative expressions impact the market less as the market gains more experience. Investors react differently and more towards negative expressions and to complex expressions, indicating that facial expressions are a rich source of information. The Fed Chair also does not consciously control his/her facial expressions, which may cause more volatility that keeps the press conference interesting and useful. Overall, there is a strong human element in the Fed Chair's delivery that may not be easily replaced by AI.

- Is Cryptocurrency Wash Trading Strategic?
  - This paper investigates the phenomenon of wash trading in cryptocurrencies, where traders simultaneously place buy and sell orders to inflate trading volumes artificially. Using high-frequency transaction data from the Mt.Gox Bitcoin exchange, I find that wash trades are strategic in response to low natural trading volume. When wash-trading in an exchange is high, the exchange's activity also mirrors on-chain transactions and other markets' transactions more closely. Wash-trading also takes place more when other asset classes trade less. Additionally, when the information environment is weak and there is more information asymmetry, wash trading's effect is slower but more sticky in the market. Wash traders also take advantage of contemporary events and induce more wash trading to boost volume. The results highlight the finding that wash trades are strategic in nature and this has implications for the financial and legal literature in allowing a better characterization of how wash trades take place, and thus, how they can be detected.
